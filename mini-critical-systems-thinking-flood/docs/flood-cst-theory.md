# Critical Systems Thinking — Flood & Jackson

## Origins
Robert L. Flood and Michael C. Jackson developed Critical Systems Thinking
(CST) at the University of Hull, UK, in the late 1980s/early 1990s. Their 1991
book *Creative Problem Solving: Total Systems Intervention* established CST as
a meta-methodology.

CST emerged from the critique that:
1. Hard systems thinking ignores human subjectivity
2. Soft systems thinking ignores power structures
3. Both may reinforce existing inequalities

## Three Core Commitments

### 1. Critical Awareness
Examining the assumptions, values, and boundary judgments that frame any
systems intervention. Key question: *Whose interests are served?*

### 2. Social Awareness
Recognizing that systems exist within societal power structures. Key
question: *What societal pressures shape this situation?*

### 3. Human Emancipation
Using systems thinking to liberate people from oppressive structures.
Key question: *How can this intervention increase human freedom?*

## Total Systems Intervention (TSI)

TSI is a meta-methodology with three phases:

### Phase 1: Creativity
Use organizational metaphors (Morgan, 1986) to surface different ways of
seeing the problem situation. Metaphors: machine, organism, brain, culture,
political system, psychic prison, flux/transformation, instrument of domination.

### Phase 2: Choice
Use the System of Systems Methodologies (SOSM) grid to select appropriate
methodologies for the problem context. Complex-coercive contexts require
emancipatory approaches.

### Phase 3: Implementation
Apply the chosen methodologies with ongoing critical reflection. Adjust
as understanding deepens.

## System of Systems Methodologies (SOSM)

The SOSM grid classifies problem contexts along two dimensions:

| | Unitary | Pluralist | Coercive |
|---|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Simple** | Hard SE/OR | SAST/SODA | CSH |
| **Complex** | SD/VSM | SSM | TSI/CST |
| **Chaotic** | Crisis Mgmt | Postmodern | Emancipatory |

## Boundary Critique (Ulrich, 1983)

Werner Ulrich's Critical Systems Heuristics (CSH) provides 12 boundary
questions organized into four categories:
- **Motivation**: Whose interests are served?
- **Control**: Who has decision power?
- **Knowledge**: What expertise counts?
- **Legitimacy**: How are affected people represented?

Boundary judgments determine what is included (facts, values) and excluded
from consideration. Marginalized voices are systematically excluded by
conventional boundary setting.
